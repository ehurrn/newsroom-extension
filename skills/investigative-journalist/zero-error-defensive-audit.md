# Zero-Error Defensive Audit

## Overview

Converts speculative narrative into procedurally defensible prose. Every sentence is audited to replace opinion, inference, and assumption with cited facts and transaction-linked data. This skill teaches you to write in a style that will survive a libel attorney's review.

## When to Use

- **Draft phase**: You have a Master File + dependency map; now you're writing for publication
- **Sensitivity review**: Senior editor or legal counsel wants to reduce litigation risk
- **High-stakes investigation**: Subject has history of legal action, or story involves powerful institutions
- **Precision requirement**: Every claim must be traceable to a specific document with a specific date

**NOT** for: Rough notes, brainstorming, or opinion pieces (those need different discipline).

## Core Pattern: Red Flag vs Green Flag Language

### The Conversion Process

Every sentence in your draft falls into one of three categories:

#### 1. Red Flags (Remove or Rewrite)

These phrases introduce libel exposure:

| Red Flag Phrase | Why It's Dangerous | Green Flag Conversion |
|-----------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| "X corrupted the board." | Accusation without evidence; invites libel suit | "Correspondence (FOIA-2024-456) shows X provided [value] to three members during review period." |
| "X appears to have benefited." | Speculation; suggests you're guessing | "X received $2.3M in contracts within six months of voting for the proposal (documented in Municipality Contract Register, 2024)." |
| "Clearly, X was trying to..." | Assumes motive; subjective interpretation | "X's voting pattern shifted after receiving donations from the interested party (FEC filing Q2-2023)." |
| "X's conflict of interest is obvious." | Legal conclusion; you're not a lawyer | "X received donations from the vendor and voted YES on the vendor's contract proposal." |
| "Multiple sources say..." | Hearsay; allows you to hide weak evidence | Cite the actual source documents. If you can't, remove the claim. |
| "Smith allegedly..." | "Allegedly" signals uncertainty; damages credibility | Use active voice: "Smith [documented action]" or conditional: "If X occurred as described in [Document], then..." |

#### 2. Green Flags (Keep and Strengthen)

These phrases anchor claims to evidence:

| Green Flag Phrase | Why It Works | Strength |
|-------------------|-------------|----------|
| "[Document name] shows..." | Pins claim to specific file | ✓✓✓ Strongest |
| "According to [official record]..." | Attributes to source; you're reporting, not asserting | ✓✓ Strong |
| "The [entity] disclosed [fact]..." | Cites official disclosure | ✓✓ Strong |
| "[Date], [actor] [action]; [Document] confirms..." | Timeline + document pairing | ✓✓ Strong |
| "Three separate documents show the same pattern..." | Convergence of evidence | ✓✓ Strong |
| "The timeline suggests [inference], though [caveat] remains unclear." | Honest inference + caveat | ✓ Moderate (for analysis sections) |

#### 3. Neutral/Conditional (Use Sparingly)

Reserved for analysis or forward-looking sections:

| Neutral Phrase | When to Use |
|---|---|
| "If [X occurred as described], then [Y] would follow." | Hypothetical analysis (use sparingly) |
| "[Document] could suggest [interpretation], though alternative explanations exist." | Analysis; not in factual sections |
| "The evidence leaves several unanswered questions." | Gap acknowledgment (honest) |

## Implementation Workflow

### Phase 1: Sentence-by-Sentence Audit

Go through your draft sentence by sentence:

1. **Identify the Claim**: What is this sentence asserting?
2. **Trace the Source**: Can you point to a specific document with a date?
3. **Classify**: Red Flag, Green Flag, or Neutral?
4. **Rewrite**: If Red Flag, convert to Green Flag or remove.

**Example Audit:**

```
DRAFT: "Smith clearly corrupted the board with his insider connections."

Claim: Smith corrupted the board
Source: ??? (No document referenced)
Classification: RED FLAG (opinion, speculation, accusation)

REWRITE: "Smith served on the board's executive committee from 2022-2024
(Corporate Governance Filing, 2024). During his tenure, board votes on
vendor contracts benefited companies where Smith held financial interests
(disclosed in SEC filing 8-K-20240315), with voting patterns showing
statistically significant alignment to Smith's known interests."

New Classification: GREEN FLAG (facts + documents + inference with caveat)
```

### Phase 2: Paragraph-Level Verification

After sentence audit, verify each paragraph's architecture:

1. **Opening Claim**: Does the paragraph's topic sentence have a documentary anchor?
2. **Supporting Detail**: Do sub-sentences reference specific documents or dates?
3. **Inference/Analysis**: If you're drawing conclusions, is it clearly marked as analysis, not fact?
4. **Closing**: Does the paragraph end with a fact, not a speculation?

**Good Paragraph Structure:**
```
[FACT with document] + [DETAIL with date] + [PATTERN across sources] +
[HONEST CAVEAT if interpretation is involved] + [CONCLUSION tied to evidence]
```

**Bad Paragraph Structure:**
```
[General claim] + [Narrative] + [Someone said...] + [Inference] +
[Speculation disguised as fact]
```

### Phase 3: Authority & Attribution Audit

Verify you're citing correctly:

| What You Wrote | What You Should Verify |
|---|---|
| "(Document Name) shows..." | Have YOU read this document? Don't cite secondhand. |
| "According to officials..." | Which officials? Can you name them or cite the statement? |
| "The agency disclosed..." | Through what mechanism? FOIA response? Public filing? Press release? Be specific. |
| "Court records show..." | Which court? Which docket number? Include the citation. |
| "The timeline suggests..." | Is this factual (timeline) or analysis (suggests)? Be clear. |

**Principle:** You can cite documents you've reviewed. You can cite official statements you've verified. You cannot cite hearsay or secondhand accounts. If it's not directly available to you, don't claim it.

### Phase 4: The Libel Scenario Test

Before finalizing, run this mental test:

**Assume the subject sues.** Now re-read your paragraph:
- Can you produce the document you cited?
- Can you defend every factual claim in front of a judge?
- Have you made any accusations without documentary backing?
- Did you confuse opinion with fact?
- Did you attribute motive or intent without evidence?

If you hesitate on any answer, rewrite.

## Quick Reference: Dangerous Constructions

| Construction | Danger | Example | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Passive voice + vague attribution** | Hides the source; implies hearsay | "It is believed that X happened" | "According to [document], X happened on [date]." |
| **Narrative flow disguised as proof** | Feels like argument, not evidence | "Given X, Y, and Z, clearly A occurred" | "Timeline: X ([doc]); Y ([doc]); Z ([doc]). This sequence raises questions about A." |
| **Adjectives doing the work** | Editorializing; not documenting | "The shocking/obvious/stunning corruption" | Remove adjectives. If the facts are strong, they don't need decoration. |
| **Implicit causation** | Suggests X caused Y without evidence | "After the donation, the official voted YES" | "The official received a donation on [date] (FEC filing). On [later date], the official voted YES (Council record). Temporal proximity raises questions about influence." |
| **Quote out of context** | Misrepresents source intent | Quote without surrounding context | Always provide context. If the quote doesn't stand up with context, don't use it. |

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|------------|-----|
| Rewriting to make narrative flow better | Introduces interpretation; strays from documents | Keep sentences close to source documents. If it reads awkward, that's often accuracy. |
| Using "sources say" as a catch-all | Allows vague sourcing; libel exposure | Name the source or cite the document. If you can't, the claim doesn't appear in the story. |
| Assuming readers will understand context | They won't; every claim must stand alone | Document every assertion independently. Assume reader has no context. |
| Conflating documents with reporters' interpretations | Cites the reporter, not the underlying source | Always cite the original document, not the article about it. |
| Treating financial flows as proof of intent | You've shown motive, not crime | Show financial flows factually; acknowledge intent questions remain unanswered. |

## Red Flags: When Your Draft Still Has Libel Exposure

| Signal | What It Means |
|--------|---------------|
| "My editor asked for a citation and I had to add it mid-review." | You drafted without documents. That's backwards. |
| "This paragraph relies on one source, and it's a news article." | You're citing a reporter's interpretation, not original documents. Go upstream. |
| "I used the word 'allegedly' or 'appears to'." | Signals uncertainty to reader and opposing counsel. Either document it or remove it. |
| "If someone reads this out of context, it sounds like an accusation." | It is. Rewrite to make context inseparable from claim. |
| "This claim sounds too perfect; like I'm building a narrative instead of reporting facts." | You are. Strip it back. Report facts; let reader draw conclusions. |
| "I'm using adjectives (shady, suspicious, clear pattern) to strengthen a weak claim." | Adjectives are proof of weak evidence. Remove them; strengthen the documentation instead. |

## Style Guide: Procedure Language

Train yourself to write procedurally. Here's the linguistic shift:

| Speculative | Procedural |
|---|---|
| "Smith is corrupt." | "Smith voted YES on three contracts benefiting vendors from which his firm received consulting fees, totaling $450K (documented in SEC filings)." |
| "The PAC clearly influences voting." | "Members voting YES on the proposal received $95K average in PAC donations; members voting NO received $12K (FEC data 2023-2024)." |
| "Smith was meeting with the vendor." | "[Date], Smith and the vendor attended the same industry conference (documented in calendar disclosure and attendee list). [Date], the vendor submitted its proposal." |
| "Everyone knows this was a setup." | "The timeline shows [X], [Y], [Z] (all documented). These facts suggest prior coordination, though direct evidence of planning remains unavailable." |

**The Pattern:** Facts + Dates + Documents → Minimal adjectives → Honest caveats about what remains unknown → Let reader conclude.

## Example Audit

**ORIGINAL DRAFT:**
"Smith's corrupt influence over the board is obvious. His insider connections allowed him to steer contracts to his cronies. Multiple sources confirm the scheme is widespread."

**AUDIT:**
- "corrupt influence" (RED): No document cited; accusation
- "insider connections" (RED): Vague; no specific evidence
- "steer contracts" (RED): Assumes intent without proof
- "multiple sources" (RED): Hearsay; no specific source

**REWRITTEN:**
"Smith served on the board's executive committee from 2022-2024 (Corporate Governance Filing, Dec 2024). During this period, the board awarded $4.2M in contracts to three vendors. Smith held financial interests in all three (disclosed in SEC filing 8-K, March 2024). Board voting records show all three contracts passed with Smith's YES vote; two passed 4-1, with the single NO vote coming from the board member who did not hold Smith's financial interests (Board Minutes, 2023-2024). These patterns raise questions about whether Smith's board position influenced vendor selection, though direct communication records between Smith and vendors remain unavailable."

**NEW CLASSIFICATION:** GREEN FLAG (facts + documents + documents-based pattern + honest caveat)
